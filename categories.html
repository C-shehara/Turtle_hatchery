SEA
TURTLE
SPECIES

Sri Lanka is home to 5 of the 7 species of sea turtles found worldwide, making it a crucial location for turtle conservation efforts. Each species has unique characteristics and behaviors, and understanding them can help us better preserve their habitats.

  1. Green turtle
  2. Olive Ridley turtle
  3. Loggerhead turtle
  4. Hawksbill turtle
  5. Leatherback turtle

1. Green Turtle

The Green Turtle is one of the largest sea turtles and is named for the greenish color of its fat.

Appearance

  • The Green Turtle can grow up to 5 feet (1.5 m) long and weigh up to 700 pounds (317 kg).
  • Its carapace is typically brown or black and is heart-shaped, with four pairs of costal scutes and a single pair of prefrontal scales.
  • The plastron is yellowish and has four pairs of inframarginal scutes.
  • Its head is small compared to its body and has a serrated beak for eating vegetation.

Behavior

  • Green Turtles are herbivores and feed mainly on seagrasses and algae.
  • They can hold their breath for up to five hours underwater.
  • During nesting season, females return to the same beach where they were born to lay their eggs.
  • Green Turtles are known for their migrations, with some individuals traveling thousands of miles between nesting and feeding grounds.

2. Olive Ridley Turtle

The Olive Ridley turtle is one of the smallest sea turtles. They are named for their olive-colored shells.

Appearance

  • Heart-shaped shell with shades of green and gray.
  • 2 feet in length
  • Weighs up to 100 pounds
  • Flattened body shape
  • Two pairs of prefrontal scales on the head

Behavior

  • Olive Ridley turtles are known for their synchronized nesting behavior, known as the arribada. Thousands of females come ashore to lay their eggs at the same time.
  • They feed on a variety of marine invertebrates and vegetation, such as crabs, shrimp, and jellyfish.
  • Olive Ridley turtles are classified as vulnerable by the IUCN due to the threats they face from human activities, including egg poaching, accidental capture in fishing gear, and habitat loss

3. Loggerhead Turtle

The loggerhead turtle is a species of sea turtle that is known for its large head and powerful jaws. They are named for their relatively large heads, which are often disproportionately large compared to their bodies.

Appearance

  • The loggerhead turtle is the largest hard-shelled turtle in the world.
  • They have a reddish-brown shell and a yellowish-brown skin with brown spots.
  • Their heads are large and have powerful jaws to crush their prey.

Behavior

  • Loggerhead turtles are known for their impressive migration patterns, traveling thousands of miles each year.
  • They are primarily carnivorous, feeding on crabs, mollusks, and other small marine creatures.
  • Like other sea turtles, loggerheads are vulnerable to habitat loss and human activity such as fishing and pollution.

4. Hawksbill Turtle

The Hawksbill turtle is a critically endangered sea turtle species that is named after its unique beak-like mouth.

Appearance

  • Medium-sized sea turtle with a distinctive beaked head and curved beak
  • Shells are heart-shaped with overlapping scutes that are brightly colored
  • Flippers have two claws each
  • Adults can weigh up to 80 kg and reach up to 1 meter in length

Behavior

  • Feed mainly on sponges, but also eat other invertebrates such as jellyfish and sea anemones
  • Nest on beaches in Sri Lanka between November and April, laying around 140 eggs per clutch
  • Hatchlings emerge after 50-70 days and make their way to the ocean
  • Hawksbill turtles are known for their incredible navigational abilities, using magnetic fields and ocean currents to find their way

5. Leatherback Turtle

The Leatherback turtle is the largest of all living turtles and is known for its unique leathery shell, which lacks the bony scutes found in other turtle species.

Appearance

  • Leatherback turtles are the largest sea turtles, measuring up to 7 feet in length and weighing up to 2000 pounds.
  • They have a unique leathery shell that lacks the bony plates found in other turtle species.
  • The shell is black or dark brown, and is covered with small white spots.

Behavior

  • Leatherback turtles are known for their long-distance migrations, sometimes traveling up to 10,000 miles in a single year.
  • They are also able to dive to depths of over 4,000 feet, making them the deepest diving of all sea turtles.
  • Leatherback turtles are primarily carnivorous, feeding on jellyfish and other soft-bodied invertebrates.
